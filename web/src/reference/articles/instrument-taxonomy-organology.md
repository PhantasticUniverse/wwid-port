# Instrument Taxonomy And Organology

## Scope

This page covers instrument naming and classification: Native American flute,
Plains flute, Woodlands flute, North American duct flute, rim-blown Indigenous
flutes, whistles, replicas, museum terms, market labels, and cultural claims.

## Agent Summary

Taxonomy is not neutral bookkeeping. A mechanism label, a maker label, a museum
label, a marketplace label, a community attribution, and a cultural-authority
claim can point at the same object while meaning different things. Agents must
keep organology, provenance, legal/product claims, and cultural source framing
separate.

Flutopedia is useful for mechanism and classification vocabulary, including
Hornbostel-Sachs and MIMO context, but it also documents disagreement around
Plains/Woodlands terms. Treat contested style labels as source-attributed,
versioned, and confidence-bounded rather than universal truth.

## Maker Workflow

1. Identify the claim type: mechanism, design family, style label, maker
   label, seller label, museum label, cultural attribution, legal claim, or
   replica claim.
2. Record the source of the label and whether it is observed, asserted,
   inferred, inherited from a catalog, or unknown.
3. Keep duct/rim-blown mechanism classification separate from Native-made,
   tribal, authentic, traditional, ceremonial, or market claims.
4. Mark Plains/Woodlands labels as contested unless a cited source provides the
   exact definition being used.
5. For restored, repaired, replicated, or derived objects, preserve original,
   current, and release labels separately.
6. Route collection, recording, community-linked, or sale-language claims
   through source-governance gates.

## Data Fields

Useful fields include object id, mechanism class, duct arrangement, chamber
count, sound mechanism, hole count, material, maker label, source label,
catalog label, market label, claimed community, claim source, classification
method, confidence, contested-term flag, restoration state, replica state,
rights tier, CARE status, IACA/legal-review status, and public wording.

## Do Not Overclaim

Do not infer Native-made, tribal, authentic, Plains, Woodlands, traditional,
ceremonial, or replica status from mechanism alone.

Do not use organology labels to launder cultural authority or marketplace
claims.

Do not collapse historical collection labels into current community-approved
language without review.

Do not silently overwrite original catalog labels after restoration, repair,
or modern adaptation.

## Open Gaps

Needed work includes a taxonomy schema, edge-case classification fixtures,
source-attributed label aliases, museum/community terminology leads, restored
object label quarantine examples, and public-wording templates that separate
mechanism, provenance, and claim authority.
