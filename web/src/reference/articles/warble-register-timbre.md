# Warble, Register, And Timbre

## Scope

This page covers warble, vibrato-induced instability, first- and
second-register behavior, squeak risk, pitch-state labels, timbre, projection,
breath noise, chiff, and audio-feature boundaries.

## Agent Summary

Warble and timbre are not single geometry knobs. They sit at the boundary of
sound-mechanism behavior, bore resonances, breath pressure, fingering, wetness,
player technique, and recording/analysis method. Flutopedia is strong for
warble terminology, spectrogram context, tuner caution, and sound-color
discussion. The research corpus turns that into engineering work: geometry
control, register hysteresis, squeak risk, timbre metrics, pitch-state labels,
and audio-feature benchmarks.

Agents should describe the observed state before assigning cause. A pitch trace
with warble, vibrato, weak fundamental, breath noise, or pitch bends is not the
same kind of evidence as a stable tuner reading from a controlled release run.

## Maker Workflow

1. Label the audio state before computing residuals: stable tone, onset,
   warble, vibrato, bend, transition, squeak, breath noise, wet-out, or
   unstable register.
2. Record fingering, breath pressure, player/fixture, environment, wet/dry
   state, block position, and recording chain.
3. Separate intentional ornament, desired warble, uncontrolled instability,
   tuner artifact, and manufacturing defect.
4. Use register claims only inside a declared fingering, pressure, and player
   envelope.
5. Pair subjective terms such as warm, breathy, bright, chiffy, airy, or
   focused with audio features and listener/measurement context.
6. Route culturally specific warble, recording, or style claims through source
   governance before public release.

## Data Fields

Useful fields include pitch-state label, note/fingering, target frequency,
pressure trace, environment, player class, fixture id, block state, wet-state
label, audio chain, sample rate, mic distance, analysis window, fundamental
confidence, spectral centroid, harmonic balance, noise/chiff metric,
modulation rate, modulation depth, register transition, and release decision.

## Do Not Overclaim

Do not average warble or vibrato into a single pitch value and call the result
proof of tuning.

Do not label warble as defect, ornament, cultural feature, or design success
without evidence for intent, source context, and measured behavior.

Do not use copyrighted recordings or culturally sensitive audio as training or
benchmark data without a compatible license or scoped rights-holder permission,
release-card approval for the exact derivative class, and CARE/community review
where relevant.

Do not generalize one flute's second-register behavior to five-hole, six-hole,
low-flute, wet-state, or adapted-instrument designs.

## Open Gaps

Needed work includes rights-cleared audio fixtures, pitch-state labels,
warble-response geometry datasets, register-transition trials, listener-linked
timbre metrics, tuner benchmark cases, and source-governed examples separating
ornament, instability, and release failure.
